## Content Strategy: Signal, Not Events

**Core principle**: Each post carries one clear signal about who the CEO is or what [Company Name] represents. Posts are not reactions to news - they're windows into the journey.

### Content Types (Mix for Authentic Persona)

| Type | What it is | Example prompt |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Journey insight** | Something we learned that wasn't obvious | "We thought X, then discovered Y" |
| **Pattern** | A repeating insight from experience | "I keep seeing the same pattern..." |
| **Decision** | A choice and why | "We decided to X even though Y" |
| **Contrast** | Expectation vs reality | "People assume X, but we've found Y" |
| **Tension** | Something unresolved | "Right now I'm balancing X and Y" |
| **Company signal** | Milestone, partnership, funding | Keep authentic, not promotional |
| **Supporting others** | Amplifying job searches, celebrating wins | Genuine support, not networking theater |
| **Location/event** | Conference or travel reflection | "I was in X and it made me think about Y" |

### How External Content Fits In

**Articles and news are topic triggers, not things to respond to.**

- Article about a relevant trend -> Topic: "[Company Name]'s experience with that trend"
- The post should be about the CEO's/company's journey with that topic
- The article/company should NOT appear in the final post
- Never frame as "This tech is interesting BUT here's the catch"

**Transform external to internal:**
- Bad: "[Company X] claims Y. But who validates the output?"
- Good: "We've been experimenting with [trend] internally. Here's what surprised us about [specific insight]."

## Post Structure Guidance

**For CEO posts, prefer:**
- Hook: Personal observation or counterintuitive claim (1-2 lines)
- Context: Brief story or experience that grounds the insight (2-3 lines)
- Core insight: The actual point, earned through experience (3-4 lines)
- Engagement: Genuine question or invitation to share perspectives (1-2 lines)

**Character target**: 500-700 characters (shorter than typical advice suggests)

**Line breaks**: Generous for readability, but NOT one-thought-per-line throughout. That creates choppy robot voice.

## Writing Style: Human, Not Optimized

**CRITICAL: Avoid "LinkedIn template" voice.** The biggest failure mode is sounding like someone following best practices instead of actually thinking.

### What kills authenticity:
- **Staccato bullet-point sentences**: "Short point. Another point. Yet another." feels robotic
- **Formulaic hooks**: "Here's the thing:" every time gets predictable
- **Over-optimized structure**: Following a rigid template makes it feel performed
- **Cramming in keywords**: Listing specifics like a checklist, not weaving them naturally

### What creates human voice:
- **Mixed sentence lengths**: Some short punches, some longer flowing thoughts
- **Conversational transitions**: "And look, I get it." / "And honestly?" / "But here's what I've learned:"
- **Reflection, not proclamation**: "Here's what I learned" beats "Here's the truth"
- **Admitting the journey**: "We almost made the same mistake" humanizes
- **Natural qualifiers**: "We're talking years, not months" / "A lot longer"

### Good example (natural flow):
```
I keep meeting startups that are 5 months old and already "enterprise-ready."

And look, I get it. The demo works. The pitch deck is polished.

But here's what I've learned: there's a canyon between a working demo and actual enterprise deployment. We're talking years, not months.

This isn't pessimism - it's just reality.
```

### Bad example (template voice):
```
Enterprise readiness is misunderstood.

Most startups think it's about the demo.

It's not.

It's about deployment.

Air-gapped networks. On-prem requirements. Security reviews.

Here's the thing: you can't fake this.
```

The first feels like someone talking. The second feels like someone performing.
