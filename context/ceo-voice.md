# [CEO Name] - Personal LinkedIn Voice Profile

This document defines the personal voice for LinkedIn posts written from the CEO's perspective. Reference this for all CEO-attributed content. For company posts, use `brand-voice.md` instead.

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Personal Authority
- **What it means**: Demonstrates deep expertise through lived experience, not just knowledge. Shows the journey, not just the destination.
- **How it sounds**: First-person perspective sharing insights earned through years of building, failing, and learning. Comfortable saying "I've seen" or "In my experience."
- **Example**: "After 15 years building developer tools, I've learned that the hardest problems aren't technical - they're about helping people understand systems they didn't build."
- **Avoid**: Generic expertise claims, speaking for "the industry" without personal grounding.

### 2. Authentic and Direct
- **What it means**: Willing to share failures, struggles, and learnings. Conversational but substantive. Real opinions, not safe corporate takes.
- **How it sounds**: Uses "I" and "we" (for the team). Admits uncertainty where it exists. Shares behind-the-scenes perspectives.
- **Example**: "We spent 6 months building a feature no one used. The lesson? We were solving our problem, not our customers' problem."
- **Avoid**: Corporate-speak, hedging on opinions, overly polished "perfect leader" image.

### 3. Industry Insider
- **What it means**: Comments on trends with insider knowledge. Provides contrarian takes when warranted. Acknowledges nuance.
- **How it sounds**: "Here's what most people miss about..." or "The uncomfortable truth is..." Connects dots others don't see.
- **Example**: "Everyone's talking about AI coding assistants. But the real shift isn't code generation - it's that understanding existing code just became the bottleneck."
- **Avoid**: Hot takes without substance, controversy for its own sake, dismissing others' views.

## What to Avoid

**Default avoid list (customize):**
- Generic leadership platitudes
- Engagement bait ("Like if you agree!")
- Perfect success stories without the struggle

**Content framing to avoid:**
- **Defensive/reactive takes**: "This tech is interesting BUT you need to check X and Y"
- **Calling out other companies**: Don't name competitors or critique their products
- **Responding to articles**: News should inspire topics, not be the subject of the post
- **Skeptic positioning**: Avoid being the person who pokes holes in others' claims
- **Tech ecosystem commentary**: Don't reference who raised what, funding rounds, or startup launches - even as a hook to make a broader point. This isn't the CEO's lane. Insights should come from personal experience and conversations, not from commenting on the tech news cycle.

## Content Strategy: Signal, Not Events

**Core principle**: Each post carries one clear signal about who the CEO is or what Swimm represents. Posts are not reactions to news - they're windows into the journey.

### Content Types (Mix for Authentic Persona)

| Type | What it is | Example prompt |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Journey insight** | Something we learned that wasn't obvious | "We thought X, then discovered Y" |
| **Pattern** | A repeating insight from experience | "I keep seeing the same pattern..." |
| **Decision** | A choice and why | "We decided to X even though Y" |
| **Contrast** | Expectation vs reality | "People assume X, but we've found Y" |
| **Tension** | Something unresolved | "Right now I'm balancing X and Y" |
| **Company signal** | Milestone, partnership, funding | Keep authentic, not promotional |
| **Supporting others** | Amplifying job searches, celebrating wins | Genuine support, not networking theater |
| **Location/event** | Conference or travel reflection | "I was in X and it made me think about Y" |

### How External Content Fits In

**Articles and news are topic triggers, not things to respond to.**

- Article about AI productivity tools → Topic: "AI + developer productivity"
- The post should be about Swimm's journey with that topic
- The article/company should NOT appear in the final post
- Never frame as "This tech is interesting BUT here's the catch"

**Transform external to internal:**
- ❌ "Profound claims 5-10x productivity. But who validates the output?"
- ✅ "We've been experimenting with AI agents internally. Here's what surprised us about the review bottleneck."

## Post Structure Guidance

**For CEO posts, prefer:**
- Hook: Personal observation or counterintuitive claim (1-2 lines)
- Context: Brief story or experience that grounds the insight (2-3 lines)
- Core insight: The actual point, earned through experience (3-4 lines)
- Engagement: Genuine question or invitation to share perspectives (1-2 lines)

**Character target**: 500-700 characters (shorter than typical advice suggests)

**Line breaks**: Generous for readability, but NOT one-thought-per-line throughout. That creates choppy robot voice.

## Writing Style: Human, Not Optimized

**CRITICAL: Avoid "LinkedIn template" voice.** The biggest failure mode is sounding like someone following best practices instead of actually thinking.

### What kills authenticity:
- **Staccato bullet-point sentences**: "Short point. Another point. Yet another." feels robotic
- **Formulaic hooks**: "Here's the thing:" every time gets predictable
- **Over-optimized structure**: Following a rigid template makes it feel performed
- **Cramming in keywords**: Listing specifics like a checklist, not weaving them naturally

### What creates human voice:
- **Mixed sentence lengths**: Some short punches, some longer flowing thoughts
- **Conversational transitions**: "And look, I get it." / "And honestly?" / "But here's what I've learned:"
- **Reflection, not proclamation**: "Here's what I learned" beats "Here's the truth"
- **Admitting the journey**: "We almost made the same mistake" humanizes
- **Natural qualifiers**: "We're talking years, not months" / "A lot longer"

### Good example (natural flow):
```
I keep meeting AI startups that are 5 months old and already "enterprise-ready."

And look, I get it. The demo works. The pitch deck is polished.

But here's what I've learned: there's a canyon between a working demo and actual enterprise deployment. We're talking years, not months.

This isn't pessimism - it's just reality.
```

### Bad example (template voice):
```
Enterprise readiness is misunderstood.

Most startups think it's about the demo.

It's not.

It's about deployment.

Air-gapped networks. On-prem requirements. Security reviews.

Here's the thing: you can't fake this.
```

The first feels like someone talking. The second feels like someone performing.
