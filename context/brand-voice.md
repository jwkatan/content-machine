# Swimm Brand Voice & Messaging

This document governs how Swimm writes across all content. It is divided into two parts:

- **Part 1: How Swimm Writes** - Universal writing style that applies to ALL content, whether or not it mentions Swimm. This is the voice, tone, and discipline that makes content recognizably "ours."
- **Part 2: What Swimm Says** - Positioning, messaging, and value propositions that apply only when Swimm is the subject or is being referenced.

When writing general thought leadership, educational content, or industry analysis that does not reference Swimm, use Part 1 only. When writing product content, landing pages, or anything that positions Swimm directly, use both parts.

# Part 1: How Swimm Writes

This section applies to everything we publish - blog posts, LinkedIn content, whitepapers, email, social media, and product copy. It defines the writing style, not the product message.

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Authoritative but Accessible
- **What it means**: We demonstrate deep expertise in legacy code and application understanding without creating distance from our audience. We're confident in our knowledge while remaining approachable.
- **How it sounds**: Professional and knowledgeable, using precise technical language when needed, but always explaining complex concepts clearly. We avoid arrogance and hedging equally.
- **Example**: "In complex legacy applications, business behavior is embedded deep in code and spread across thousands of files, jobs, and data flows. Over time, understanding fragments across people, teams, and past decisions, leaving critical knowledge incomplete, outdated, or lost."
- **Avoid**: Hedging language ("might," "could," "perhaps"), arrogant positioning, dumbing down complex topics, or oversimplifying technical realities.

### 2. Direct and Active
- **What it means**: We use active voice, punchy sentences, and clear agency. We get to the point quickly and respect our audience's time.
- **How it sounds**: Short, declarative sentences mixed with substantive explanations. Strong verbs. No fluff or padding. Varied rhythm to maintain readability.
- **Example**: "Existing approaches force teams into either people-heavy manual analysis across many tools, or LLM-based analysis that explains code file-by-file without establishing a complete, end-to-end view of application behavior. Teams get fast answers, but no full picture."
- **Avoid**: Passive voice, long meandering paragraphs, hedging, weasel words, fluff, three or more consecutive short sentences that feel choppy, arbitrarily splitting sentences when one larger sentence communicates better.

### 3. Professional B2B Without Jargon
- **What it means**: We maintain a professional tone appropriate for enterprise audiences while actively avoiding corporate speak and marketing manipulation.
- **How it sounds**: Respectful of reader intelligence, technically precise, but preferring simple, concise, clear language. We say "challenge," "cost," and "risk" instead of marketing abstractions.
- **Example**: "Accurate understanding must be reconstructed under scrutiny. Interviews and workshops are slow, person-dependent, and hard to converge. Structural analysis describes structure, not behavior. AI summaries operate on partial context and inference."
- **Avoid**: Marketing-speak ("synergize," "leverage," "disrupt," "innovate"), fear-based language ("nightmare," "disaster," "crisis"), competitive framing ("join the X% that succeed"), superlatives without evidence ("revolutionary," "game-changing"), emotional manipulation.

### 4. Customer-Centric
- **What it means**: Our customers are the heroes, never the problem. We acknowledge their challenges and complexity without implying they did something wrong.
- **How it sounds**: We frame problems as industry challenges, not customer failures. We focus on reducing execution risk rather than "catching" mistakes. We celebrate their expertise and help them succeed.
- **Example**: "Reduce execution risk by making dependencies, constraints, and preserved behavior explicit" not "Catch what you would have missed."
- **Avoid**: Language that implies customer error, blame-oriented framing, patronizing explanations, positioning ourselves as the hero instead of the enabler.

## Voice Principles

These four principles govern the character of our writing across all content types.

### Grounded in Facts
We describe what applications do, based on code-derived evidence. We avoid opinions, aspirations, and future-state promises. Even in thought leadership, we anchor claims in observable reality - how systems actually work, what teams actually face, what approaches actually produce.

### Clear Under Scrutiny
We write for leaders making high-risk change decisions. Language should reduce uncertainty by making behavior, scope, and dependencies explicit. If a reader is using our content to inform a decision they will need to defend, our writing should hold up.

### Precise by Default
We use concrete, unambiguous terms. If a word can be interpreted more than one way, we replace it. "Application behavior" is better than "legacy insights." "Execution risk" is better than "challenges ahead." Precision builds trust; vagueness erodes it.

### Continuous, Not One-Time
Understanding is not a planning-stage input that gets consumed once and filed away. It is relied on at every stage of a modernization program - assessment, planning, requirements engineering, forward engineering, and test/deploy/migrate. Write with this in mind. Avoid framing that implies understanding is only relevant "before" action begins.

## Tone Guidelines

### Default Tone: Confident Expert Colleague
Imagine you're an experienced enterprise architect or senior technical leader helping a peer succeed. You know legacy systems deeply, you understand the real challenges they face, and you provide specific, actionable guidance. You're confident without being arrogant, technical without being inaccessible, and direct without being curt. You respect your audience's intelligence and time constraints, so you get to the point with clarity and substance.

This is the default tone for educational content, thought leadership, how-to guides, and general industry writing.

### Elevated Tone: Authority Under Scrutiny
For executive-facing content, partner proposals, and anything where Swimm is being positioned for high-stakes decisions, elevate to a more deliberate register. The audience is approving multi-million dollar change programs. Language should be grounded, precise, and defensible. Less conversational warmth, more weight and evidence.

Use this tone for: product positioning, executive briefs, partner/GSI materials, case studies, and content that directly supports buying decisions.

### Tone Variations by Content Type

**How-To Guides & Educational Content**: Direct, instructional, technically specific. Default tone.
- "Reverse engineer application behavior by analyzing execution paths across the complete codebase, not just individual programs."
- "Map dependencies across your codebase to understand system flows and integration points."
- "Capture institutional knowledge from code before engineers leave, making it accessible independent of individual availability."

**Strategy/Advice Content**: Authoritative, substantive, frameworks-oriented. Default tone with more depth.
- "Modernization programs stall when understanding becomes the bottleneck. Teams cannot commit to change they cannot accurately describe."
- "Knowledge gaps emerge from three sources: retiring staff, deteriorated documentation, and compounding complexity in decades-old systems."
- "Establish accurate understanding of application behavior to reduce execution risk - from initial assessment through forward engineering and migration."

**Industry News/Trends**: Analytical, specific, evidence-based. Default tone.
- "Legacy applications carry institutional knowledge in code, not documentation. When engineers leave, that knowledge disappears."
- "Enterprise teams spend 60% of development time understanding unfamiliar code rather than delivering features."
- "Existing approaches force a choice between people-heavy manual analysis and LLM-based summaries that explain code without establishing complete application understanding."

**Product/Feature Content**: Benefit-focused, specific, technically credible. Elevated tone.
- "Establish accurate, code-derived understanding of how legacy applications behave. Review and verify in minutes instead of weeks."
- "A single, trusted understanding that teams and tools rely on across planning, engineering, and delivery."
- "Deterministic analysis derived directly from source code. No inference, no guessing, no probabilistic behavior modeling."

**AI & Forward Engineering Content**: Technically grounded, outcome-focused. Default or elevated tone depending on audience.
- "Accurate application understanding becomes the trusted context that AI tools and agents rely on for forward engineering."
- "AI-driven code generation and modernization execution require complete, verified understanding of existing behavior to produce reliable results."
- "Swimm provides the understanding layer. AI consumes it for accurate code generation, migration, and transformation."

**Partner/GSI Content**: Professional, precise, respectful of delivery ownership. Elevated tone.
- "Converge on accurate system understanding earlier, with fewer people and less rework, without impacting delivery ownership or methodology."
- "Establish the understanding baseline that reduces execution risk across the program lifecycle."
- "Complement existing discovery frameworks with accurate, code-derived understanding that persists across planning and delivery."

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones to create good rhythm. Avoid three or more consecutive short sentences that create a choppy feeling.
- **Active voice preferred**: "Senior engineers review code line-by-line" not "Code is reviewed line-by-line by senior engineers"
- **Average length**: Adjust to medium - shorter for email, longer substantive sentences acceptable for whitepapers
- **Clarity first**: Never sacrifice clarity for brevity. Use one larger sentence if it communicates better than arbitrarily splitting into smaller ones.

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2-3 sentences typically
- **One idea per paragraph**: Each paragraph focuses on a single concept or point
- **Transitions**: Logical flow between paragraphs, connecting ideas naturally
- **White space**: Use paragraph breaks generously to improve visual readability and scanability
- **No restating**: State each idea once in its strongest form. If two sections cover the same ground, consolidate them.

### Word Choice
- **Specific and concrete**: Use "40% improvement" not "better performance," give real-world examples and concrete scenarios
- **Active verbs**: "Establish," "Derive," "Extract," "Verify," "Capture," "Reduce," "Align," "Converge," "Eliminate"
- **No hedging**: Be direct. Avoid "might," "could," "perhaps," "potentially"
- **Avoid fluff**: Cut "very," "really," "actually," "simply" unless truly needed. No padding or filler content.
- **No marketing-speak**: Avoid "synergize," "leverage," "disrupt," "innovate," "transform," "digital innovation," "unlock," "empower"
- **No fear language**: Replace "nightmare," "disaster," "crisis," "catastrophically" with "challenge," "cost," "risk," "significant"

### Terminology

**Say This** -> **Not That**
- Legacy code -> Old code, outdated code (More precise and respectful)
- Application understanding -> Documentation, code knowledge (Broader, more accurate concept)
- Application behavior -> Code functionality, system operations (Precise term for what applications actually do)
- Knowledge capture -> Documentation creation (Emphasizes preserving existing knowledge)
- Tribal knowledge -> Undocumented knowledge (Industry-standard term for information only in people's heads)
- Enterprise teams -> Enterprise clients, large organizations (More respectful, less transactional)
- Knowledge gap -> Documentation gap (Captures the full scope: people + docs + complexity)
- Business rule extraction (BRE) -> Rule extraction (Full technical term on first use)
- Reverse engineering -> Code analysis (When used in technical, accurate context)
- Execution risk -> Challenges ahead, potential issues (Precise and concrete)
- Code-derived understanding -> AI-powered insights (Understanding is factual, not inferred)

### Using "Application Behavior"

"Application behavior" is our canonical term for what Swimm produces understanding of. It means: what the application actually does - the executable business rules, decision logic, data flows, processing sequences, and business processes as implemented in code. It deliberately excludes structural inventory, code quality metrics, and technical debt assessment.

**Important**: "Application behavior" leans technical. When writing for business-oriented audiences (business analysts, program managers, business stakeholders), expand the term to make the business dimension explicit:
- "Application behavior - including business rules, decision logic, and business processes as implemented in code"
- "Business and technical behavior"
- "Business rules and application behavior"

For technical audiences (architects, engineers), "application behavior" alone is sufficient and precise.

### Vocabulary Guardrails

**Preferred language** (use freely):
- Accurate understanding
- Application behavior
- Business logic as implemented
- Code-derived
- Evidence-backed
- Traceable
- Trusted reference
- Execution risk
- Scalable analysis

**Allowed with care** (use when factually accurate, not as marketing):
- Faster alignment on understanding
- Reduced time to understanding
- Automation makes accurate understanding practical at enterprise scale

**AI language rules:**
- When positioning Swimm's primary value (H1, brand positioning, core UVP), lead with understanding and outcomes, not AI
- AI is a legitimate and important topic for articles, features, and use cases - especially AI-driven forward engineering, where Swimm's understanding serves as trusted context for AI tools and agents
- The key distinction: AI does not derive or validate Swimm's understanding of application behavior (that is deterministic). AI consumes and translates that understanding - and downstream AI tools rely on it for accurate forward engineering.
- Approved phrasings: "AI is used to deliver understanding in human-readable form," "Downstream AI tools rely on this understanding as trusted context," "Accurate understanding becomes the foundation for AI-driven forward engineering"
- Avoid positioning Swimm *itself* as: "AI-powered," "AI-driven," "AI-first," "autonomous," or "agentic"

**Technical Practitioners** (secondary reader, primary for educational content):

**Partner/GSI Stakeholders** (reader for partner-facing content):

### What They Care About

**Top Priorities**:
1. Predictable timelines and controlled costs for modernization and change programs
2. Establishing accurate understanding that holds up from assessment through forward engineering and migration
3. Converging on a single, trusted understanding across teams and vendors
4. Preserving institutional knowledge before it is lost
5. Traceable evidence for compliance, audit, and stakeholder sign-off

**Pain Points**:
1. Understanding is fragmented across individuals and teams, and much of it has been lost over time
2. Delayed convergence on how applications behave
3. Elevated risk when committing to change without accurate understanding
4. Scope expansion as understanding evolves during execution
5. Misalignment across teams and vendors
6. Late scope changes, rework, and defects emerging during testing and cutover

### How to Serve Them

- **Respect their time**: Get to the point quickly with no fluff or padding
- **Make it actionable**: Provide specific, implementable frameworks and processes, not vague advice
- **Acknowledge complexity**: Show we understand their work is genuinely difficult, don't oversimplify technical realities
- **Use quantified evidence**: Give specific metrics (minutes vs. weeks, 60% time savings) not vague improvements
- **Demonstrate domain authority**: Use precise terminology, real-world examples, and technical credibility that respects their expertise
- **Be specific about what fails**: When framing the problem, name the specific approach that falls short and explain why rather than using vague "challenges" language

## Content Formatting

### Headlines
- **Always sentence case**: Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. "How to extract business rules from legacy code in 2026" not "How to Extract Business Rules from Legacy Code in 2026"
- **Be specific**: "How to extract business rules from legacy code in 2026" not "Business rule tips"
- **Include benefit**: Tell readers what they'll gain - reduced risk, faster alignment, accurate understanding
- **Use numbers**: "7 Ways to Reduce Execution Risk" or "Complete Guide to Application Understanding"
- **Interest-based for blogs**: "What is [Topic]?" or "How do I [Action]?" or "Best [Topic] tools for 2026"

### Subheadings
- **Always sentence case**: Same rule as headlines - capitalize only the first word and proper nouns
- **Use logical hierarchy**: H1 -> H2s -> H3s, never skip levels
- **Make them descriptive**: Subheadings should convey key information, not just labels
- **Front-load keywords**: Put important terms at the beginning for scannability
- **Keep concise**: Short enough to scan quickly while conveying the section's content

### Lists
- **Numbered lists**: Use for processes, sequential steps, tips, enumerated items
- **Bullet points**: Use for feature lists only, not for processes or steps
- **Parallel structure**: Keep list items grammatically consistent
- **Specific items**: Each item should be concrete and actionable, not vague

### Calls-to-Action
- **Request-focused**: "Request a demo with Swimm" not "Sign up now!"
- **Clear next step**: Tell readers exactly what to do next
- **Benefit-oriented**: Connect CTA to value they'll receive
- **Professional tone**: No urgency manipulation or artificial scarcity

# Part 2: What Swimm Says

This section applies only when Swimm is the subject or is being referenced. It defines positioning and value propositions. Use this alongside Part 1's writing style.

## Positioning

### What Swimm Is
Swimm is an Application Understanding Platform. It produces accurate, code-derived understanding of mainframe and legacy applications - for humans and AI to modernize with confidence.

### What Swimm Is Not
Swimm is not an end-to-end modernization tool and it is not a documentation platform.

### Swimm and AI
Swimm is AI-enabled, but AI is not the source of truth for application understanding. The distinction:
- **Understanding** (what Swimm produces): Derived deterministically from source code. Traceable, reviewable, evidence-backed. AI does not infer this.
- **Translation** (how understanding is delivered): AI translates deterministic findings into human-readable form in customer business language.
- **Forward engineering** (how understanding is consumed): Swimm's understanding serves as trusted context for AI tools, agents, and platforms that drive code generation, migration, and modernization execution. This is a core part of Swimm's value - accurate understanding makes AI-driven forward engineering reliable.

Writing about AI and Swimm is encouraged when the topic is how accurate understanding enables better AI outcomes. Avoid positioning Swimm's core analysis as AI-driven.

### How Accuracy Is Achieved
Use this framing when trust or defensibility is questioned. The mechanism supports the value; it does not replace it.

Swimm achieves accuracy through:
- Deterministic analysis derived directly from source code
- Traceable, reviewable understanding of application behavior
- LLMs used only to translate understanding into human-readable form
- No inference, guessing, or probabilistic behavior modeling

## Message Sequencing

When structuring Swimm-specific content, follow this sequence. Lead with what matters most to the reader, then build trust.

| Layer | Purpose | Core Message | Avoid |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Outcome** (lead) | Establish relevance | Clear, trusted understanding that enables confident progress on critical change | Technology, AI, feature language |
| **Risk** (second) | Frame execution confidence | Reducing execution risk by making dependencies, constraints, and preserved behavior explicit | Productivity or delivery framing |
| **Mechanism** (last) | Build trust | Deterministic, code-grounded understanding. LLMs translate; they do not infer. | AI-first or autonomous framing |

Do NOT lead with mechanism (how Swimm works technically). Lead with the outcome the audience cares about, then frame the risk Swimm addresses, then explain the mechanism only when trust or defensibility is questioned.

**Exception**: Content specifically about AI and forward engineering can lead with the AI topic. The sequencing rule applies to Swimm's primary positioning, not to every article topic.

## GSI/Partner Context

Swimm's go-to-market is predominantly partner-led. When writing partner-facing or partner-adjacent content:

- **Respect delivery ownership**: Swimm does not own execution, replace GSI methodology, or compete with delivery services
- **Frame as enablement**: Swimm removes uncertainty from application understanding without altering how partners scope, plan, or deliver
- **Emphasize independence**: Understanding persists independently of the delivery approach and is contracted separately
- **Focus on convergence**: Partners include Swimm because it enables faster alignment on a single, trusted understanding
