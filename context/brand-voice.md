# Part 1: How [Company Name] Writes

This section applies to everything we publish - blog posts, LinkedIn content, whitepapers, email, social media, and product copy. It defines the writing style, not the product message.

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Authoritative but Accessible
<!-- Instructions: Replace the example with a paragraph from your own domain that demonstrates expertise without arrogance. -->
- **What it means**: We demonstrate deep expertise in our domain without creating distance from our audience. We're confident in our knowledge while remaining approachable.
- **How it sounds**: Professional and knowledgeable, using precise technical language when needed, but always explaining complex concepts clearly. We avoid arrogance and hedging equally.
- **Example**: "In complex engineering organizations, code review bottlenecks silently erode velocity. Pull requests stack up, context decays while waiting for reviewers, and the feedback that eventually arrives is often superficial - catching style nits while missing architectural risks."
- **Avoid**: Hedging language ("might," "could," "perhaps"), arrogant positioning, dumbing down complex topics, or oversimplifying technical realities.

### 2. Direct and Active
- **What it means**: We use active voice, punchy sentences, and clear agency. We get to the point quickly and respect our audience's time.
- **How it sounds**: Short, declarative sentences mixed with substantive explanations. Strong verbs. No fluff or padding. Varied rhythm to maintain readability.
- **Example**: "Existing approaches force teams into either slow, manual code reviews that block the entire pipeline, or superficial automated checks that miss the defects that matter most. Teams get speed or quality, but rarely both."
- **Avoid**: Passive voice, long meandering paragraphs, hedging, weasel words, fluff, three or more consecutive short sentences that feel choppy, arbitrarily splitting sentences when one larger sentence communicates better.

### 3. Professional B2B Without Jargon
- **What it means**: We maintain a professional tone appropriate for enterprise audiences while actively avoiding corporate speak and marketing manipulation.
- **How it sounds**: Respectful of reader intelligence, technically precise, but preferring simple, concise, clear language. We say "challenge," "cost," and "risk" instead of marketing abstractions.
- **Example**: "Manual code review doesn't scale. Senior engineers spend hours reviewing pull requests instead of building. Automated linters catch formatting issues but miss logic errors. Neither approach gives teams confidence that production code is safe."
- **Avoid**: Marketing-speak ("synergize," "leverage," "disrupt," "innovate"), fear-based language ("nightmare," "disaster," "crisis"), competitive framing ("join the X% that succeed"), superlatives without evidence ("revolutionary," "game-changing"), emotional manipulation.

### 4. Customer-Centric
- **What it means**: Our customers are the heroes, never the problem. We acknowledge their challenges and complexity without implying they did something wrong.
- **How it sounds**: We frame problems as industry challenges, not customer failures. We focus on improving outcomes rather than "catching" mistakes. We celebrate their expertise and help them succeed.
- **Example**: "Reduce review cycle time by surfacing the changes that matter most" not "Stop missing critical bugs in your code reviews."
- **Avoid**: Language that implies customer error, blame-oriented framing, patronizing explanations, positioning ourselves as the hero instead of the enabler.

## Voice Principles

These four principles govern the character of our writing across all content types.

### Grounded in Facts
We describe what our product does and what our industry faces, based on evidence. We avoid opinions, aspirations, and future-state promises. Even in thought leadership, we anchor claims in observable reality - how systems actually work, what teams actually face, what approaches actually produce.

### Clear Under Scrutiny
We write for leaders making important technical and business decisions. Language should reduce uncertainty by making trade-offs, scope, and capabilities explicit. If a reader is using our content to inform a decision they will need to defend, our writing should hold up.

### Precise by Default
We use concrete, unambiguous terms. If a word can be interpreted more than one way, we replace it. Precision builds trust; vagueness erodes it.

### Continuous, Not One-Time
<!-- Instructions: Adapt this principle to your product's ongoing value story. -->
Our product delivers ongoing value, not a one-time fix. Write with this in mind. Avoid framing that implies our value is only relevant at a single point in the workflow.

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones to create good rhythm. Avoid three or more consecutive short sentences that create a choppy feeling.
- **Active voice preferred**: "Senior engineers review code line-by-line" not "Code is reviewed line-by-line by senior engineers"
- **Average length**: Adjust to medium - shorter for email, longer substantive sentences acceptable for whitepapers
- **Clarity first**: Never sacrifice clarity for brevity. Use one larger sentence if it communicates better than arbitrarily splitting into smaller ones.

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2-3 sentences typically
- **One idea per paragraph**: Each paragraph focuses on a single concept or point
- **Transitions**: Logical flow between paragraphs, connecting ideas naturally
- **White space**: Use paragraph breaks generously to improve visual readability and scanability
- **No restating**: State each idea once in its strongest form. If two sections cover the same ground, consolidate them.

### Word Choice
- **Specific and concrete**: Use "40% improvement" not "better performance," give real-world examples and concrete scenarios
- **Active verbs**: "Identify," "Surface," "Flag," "Route," "Measure," "Reduce," "Automate," "Integrate," "Streamline"
- **No hedging**: Be direct. Avoid "might," "could," "perhaps," "potentially"
- **Avoid fluff**: Cut "very," "really," "actually," "simply" unless truly needed. No padding or filler content.
- **No marketing-speak**: Avoid "synergize," "leverage," "disrupt," "innovate," "transform," "digital innovation," "unlock," "empower"
- **No fear language**: Replace "nightmare," "disaster," "crisis," "catastrophically" with "challenge," "cost," "risk," "significant"

## Content Formatting

### Headlines
- **Always sentence case**: Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns.
- **Be specific**: "How to reduce code review cycle time by 60%" not "Code review tips"
- **Include benefit**: Tell readers what they'll gain
- **Use numbers**: "7 ways to speed up pull request reviews" or "Complete guide to code review automation"
- **Interest-based for blogs**: "What is [Topic]?" or "How do I [Action]?" or "Best [Topic] tools for [Year]"

### Subheadings
- **Always sentence case**: Same rule as headlines - capitalize only the first word and proper nouns
- **Use logical hierarchy**: H1 -> H2s -> H3s, never skip levels
- **Make them descriptive**: Subheadings should convey key information, not just labels
- **Front-load keywords**: Put important terms at the beginning for scannability
- **Keep concise**: Short enough to scan quickly while conveying the section's content

### Lists
- **Numbered lists**: Use for processes, sequential steps, tips, enumerated items
- **Bullet points**: Use for feature lists only, not for processes or steps
- **Parallel structure**: Keep list items grammatically consistent
- **Specific items**: Each item should be concrete and actionable, not vague

### Calls-to-Action
<!-- Instructions: Replace these CTAs with your own. -->
- **Request-focused**: "Request a demo with [Company Name]" not "Sign up now!"
- **Clear next step**: Tell readers exactly what to do next
- **Benefit-oriented**: Connect CTA to value they'll receive
