## Narrative Strategy

### The conviction arc

This page builds a single argument across eleven sections. The argument is: **understanding is the foundational layer of modernization, Swimm owns that layer, and nothing else produces understanding at this level of accuracy, completeness, or enterprise readiness.**

Each section advances one stage of buyer conviction. No section repeats the job of another. The scroll sequence mirrors the buyer's decision process: from "What is this?" to "Can I defend choosing this?"

### Message sequencing rule

Every section that describes Swimm follows the mandatory sequence:
1. **Outcome first** - what changes for the buyer
2. **Risk second** - what risk is reduced or eliminated
3. **Mechanism last** - how Swimm accomplishes it (deterministic, code-derived analysis; AI translates, does not infer)

No section leads with mechanism. No section describes how Swimm works before establishing why the buyer should care.

### Audience assumptions

The primary reader is a CIO, SVP of IT, or modernization program owner. They are:
- Skeptical of AI claims. They have been burned by tools that promise AI magic.
- Time-constrained. They scan headlines, read selectively, and judge in seconds.
- Accountable for high-risk change decisions worth tens of millions of dollars.
- Looking for defensible evidence, not marketing energy.

**Must-do**: Name the specific consequences of modernizing without accurate application understanding. Frame Swimm's reliability as the elimination of a defined risk, not as a repeated claim of deterministic accuracy (which Section 2 already established).

**Supporting content direction**:

  2. **Inaccurate dependencies cause cascade failures**: Incomplete dependency mapping means changes propagate in ways no one predicted. Swimm's code-derived dependency analysis eliminates this blind spot.

Errors compound silently through generated artifacts. This is the failure consequence, not the positive framing of Section 3 (which establishes that Swimm provides trusted context). **Writer guardrail**: This point must emphasize the FAILURE cascade — what goes wrong when AI operates on bad context — rather than restating Section 3's argument that Swimm provides the trusted context layer. The distinction: Section 3 says "AI works better with Swimm." This point says "AI fails dangerously without accurate context."

**What to avoid**:

- Attacking AI or LLMs in general
- Marketing superlatives ("unparalleled," "guaranteed")
- Restating Section 3's positive framing ("Swimm provides the trusted context layer") in point 3 — instead, emphasize the failure consequence of operating without accurate context

## Narrative Arc Assessment

### How the page builds conviction

The page follows a deliberate persuasion sequence that mirrors how a skeptical CIO evaluates a new category:

**Phase 1 - Outcome and Category Definition (Sections 1-2)**
"What outcome does this deliver, and why should I pay attention?"
The hero opens with the buyer's desired outcome — confident modernization decisions — and names the gap between ambition and actual knowledge of what applications do. The differentiation section immediately proves this is not another AI summary tool — it produces specific, valuable output (business rules, dependencies, decision paths) derived directly from source code. These two sections establish the outcome, define the category, and separate Swimm from everything the buyer has already dismissed.

**Phase 2 - Strategic Relevance (Sections 3-4, including risk beat)**
"How does this fit into what we're already doing?"
The AI-ready modernization section addresses the buyer's most pressing strategic question: AI tools are already in play, and Swimm feeds them better context. The risk beat names the cost of proceeding without accurate understanding. The unified understanding section broadens the value to every stakeholder, not just AI tools. By the end of Section 4, the buyer understands: this is not a point solution, it is the understanding layer for the entire modernization program.

**Phase 3 - Social Proof and Substance (Sections 4b, 5, 6)**
"Who else trusts this, what do I get, and what risk does it eliminate?"
Customer logos provide named social proof. The features section demonstrates scope and completeness. The risk-mitigation section names the specific failure modes of modernizing without accurate understanding. These three sections convert interest into confidence.

**Phase 4 - Qualification (Sections 7-9)**
"Can I actually buy this?"
Enterprise viability, technical coverage, and Gartner category validation remove procurement-level objections. These are qualification checkboxes, not persuasion sections. They should feel like a fact sheet, not marketing copy.

**Phase 5 - Conversion (Section 10)**
"What's my next step?"
The final section distills the entire argument into one headline and one button. No new information. No new claims. Just confidence and a clear path forward.

### Risks to monitor during content creation

1. **Mechanism creep**: The Writer must resist the urge to explain how Swimm works before establishing why the buyer cares. Outcome first, risk second, mechanism last - in every section.
2. **Word budget discipline**: At ~700-750 words total, every word must earn its place. The Editor should enforce hard maximums per section.
3. **Tone drift**: The elevated, authority-under-scrutiny tone can easily slip into either marketing energy (too warm) or academic density (too cold). The Writer should aim for the register of a board presentation, not a blog post and not a whitepaper.
4. **AI positioning violations**: Swimm must never be described as "AI-powered" or "AI-driven." The distinction between deterministic analysis (what Swimm does) and AI translation (how findings become human-readable) must remain crisp in every section.
5. **Forbidden vocabulary**: The Editor must flag any instance of: unlock, empower, transform, revolutionize, cutting-edge, next-generation, seamless, AI-powered, AI-driven, AI-first, synergize, leverage, disrupt, innovate, end-to-end.
6. **Section 2/6 redundancy**: The Editor must verify that Section 6 addresses failure modes and risk mitigation, NOT restating the deterministic-vs.-inferred differentiation that Section 2 owns.
7. **Jargon grounding (BBQ test)**: The Editor must flag any instance of "specification-grade understanding," "behavioral reconstruction," or "code-derived understanding" that appears in copy without being grounded in what it produces. These terms must always be expanded or replaced with outcome language per Directive 3.
8. **Application behavior expansion**: The Editor must verify that every CIO-facing headline, key message, and subheadline expands "application behavior" to include business rules, decision logic, and business processes per brand voice rules. The bare term is only acceptable in technical-audience contexts.
9. **Section 3/6 point 3 boundary**: The Editor must verify that Section 6 point 3 (AI context risk) emphasizes the failure consequence, not a restatement of Section 3's positive framing. Section 3 says "AI works better with Swimm." Section 6 point 3 says "AI fails dangerously without accurate context."
